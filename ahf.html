AI, Humanity, & The Future

Designed and deployed a cultural probe to explore public perceptions towards AI

Role UX Researcher
Timeline 6 weeks | ACM CSCW Poster
Team 4 designers
Deliverables Cultural probe kit, documentation of research insights

Overview

It feels like it’s everywhere. Conversations about Artificial Intelligence – what it is, when to use it, and how it will affect us – are occurring anywhere from the daily news to interpersonal conversations. Communities are responding in varying ways and grappling with a flurry of differing opinions. This pilot study explores a unique methodology involving the deployment of a cultural probe, which allowed us to understand public perceptions and lived experiences with AI that may not be reflected in large aggregate datasets. Building on these insights, future studies utilizing this specific cultural probe design may further explore how public perceptions of technology range across populations and facilitate reflection on the effects of emerging technologies such as AI on users and humanity in general.

Probe Components

The probe consisted of two activities: (1) A physical “Outlook Journal” or reflective journal, and (2) A “Digital Polaroid” website that allows one to take photos using their smartphone. Our team designed and hand bound the Outlook Journals and instruction cards and built the Digital Polaroid website! The two activities of our probe work in tandem to allow for participants to engage in different contexts and modes of reflection, an approach that has been seen in other cultural probes. The physical components of the probe were mailed to participants. The activities in the cultural probe were to be completed over the course of five days, encouraging the completion of both activities each day. Eight participants were recruited and participated in the pilot study: five were from a technology field and three were from other fields.

Outlook Journal

The physical Outlook Journal was kept by each subject and taken with participants throughout their day. Participants were asked to write down their thoughts and feelings of hopefulness and hopelessness regarding AI. The physicality of the Outlook Journal allowed participants to slow down and reflect through written or drawn input on their feelings and thoughts about AI.

This component is based on diary studies, a qualitative ethnographic research method where participants note their thoughts over a period of time. This method allows researchers to access in-context experiences without being physically present and potentially biasing participants. The privateness of reflecting in a journal clearly came out in the participants’ entries, which ranged from vulnerable to impassioned to critical, and highlights the value of using this over other forms of research for large, socially involved issues.

Digital Polaroid

The second activity was completed through the Digital Polaroid website, which mimicked a digital camera. Participants took pictures of their surroundings whenever they encountered something that reminded them of AI or made them consider its impact on their lives. After taking a photo, they selected a prompt describing how the contents of the image might be affected by AI and could also include additional comments.

The Digital Polaroid served as a convenient way for people to take photos. While other cultural probes included disposable film cameras, we chose to use a website over a film camera, as participants may be more likely to carry their phones with them through the day. We designed the website with limited forms of input and capture in an attempt to encourage participants to quickly take pictures without overanalyzing the aesthetics of the photo. Instead, limiting the interaction to a shutter button and prompt input was decided in an effort to focus on conveying the thoughts that prompted them to take the photo.

Findings

Participants sent our team scanned pages of their Outlook Journal. We analyzed the data from the probes by creating semantic and latent codes. Using Voyant Tools, we visualized the data and created a word cloud.

Key Insights

Human-ness is important to people in regards to AI.

A little under a third of the “hopeful” pages were left completely blank, showing that AI evokes more hopeless rather than hopeful feelings in participants.

Participants had varying levels of technical knowledge and were able to participate and provide rich reflections, observations, and thoughts on AI's potential impacts, highlighting how this is a nuanced subject that impacts everyone.

The probe served as a conversation starter for participants and allowed them to have conversations with friends and family about their thoughts on AI. This conversational and collaborative element is especially interesting, as it sparked new forms of understanding AI.

The probe also revealed a number of deeper, messier emotions, such as a general lack of fulfillment when heavily relying on AI to complete tasks. Several participants also noticed that they exhibited lowered engagement and active participation during classes or meetings when AI generated summaries and notes were available to them. These cognitive and emotional impacts on learning, education, and work require further research.

What I Learned

Benefits of the Cultural Probe as a Research Method

The probe gave light to a number of insights that might have been harder to glean from a simpler interview or survey that lack the contextual situatedness of a probe. By providing participants with a submission method that is portable and accessible at their own discretion, participants were able to contemplate the prompts given to provoke ideas about how AI might affect our collective future, including in the areas of life that are most salient to them. The probe was a novel method of facilitated reflection; the activities provided enough guidance to support reflecting in different ways and in different contexts but avoided large-scale steering of the participant, permitting for a full breadth of answers that may otherwise be missed with a traditional form of user research. As technology rapidly advances, there is a need for critical reflection on our relationship with technology, its role(s) in our lives, and the ways in which it impacts us. Access to these in-context, personal narratives provide essential understandings of how emerging technologies are being understood by a variety of people and are strongly situated to inform future design, research, and policy decisions as emerging technologies evolve.
